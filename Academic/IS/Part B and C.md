# Earthing, Earthing Resistance and Reading Earth Tester (20 Marks Answer)

---

# Introduction

Electricity is widely used in homes, industries, and power systems. However, improper handling of electrical systems can cause electric shock, fire, and equipment damage. Earthing is one of the most important safety methods used in electrical systems to protect humans and equipment from electrical hazards.

Earthing provides a safe path for fault current to flow into the ground, thereby preventing electric shock and ensuring safe operation of electrical systems.

---

---

# Definition of Earthing

Earthing is the process of connecting the metal parts of electrical equipment to the earth using a low resistance conductor.

In simple words, earthing connects electrical equipment to the ground to prevent electric shock.

---

# Objectives of Earthing

The main objectives of earthing are:

• To protect human beings from electric shock  
• To provide safe path for fault current  
• To protect electrical equipment  
• To prevent fire accidents  
• To maintain voltage stability

---

# Working Principle of Earthing

Under normal conditions, electric current flows through the circuit.

But when insulation fails, fault current flows through metal body.

Earthing provides a low resistance path to ground.

As a result:

• Current flows safely into ground  
• Prevents electric shock  
• Protects equipment

---

# Types of Earthing

There are three main types of earthing:

---

## 1. Plate Earthing

In this method, a metal plate is buried in the ground.

Materials used:

• Copper plate  
• GI (Galvanized Iron) plate

Depth: 3 meters below ground

Advantages:

• Good conductivity  
• Reliable

---

## 2. Pipe Earthing (Most Common)

In this method, a GI pipe is buried vertically in ground.

Advantages:

• Low cost  
• Easy installation  
• Most efficient

Used in:

• Houses  
• Industries

---

## 3. Rod Earthing

In this method, a copper or steel rod is driven into ground.

Advantages:

• Simple method  
• Used in small installations

---

# Definition of Earthing Resistance

Earthing resistance is the resistance offered by earth electrode to the flow of electric current into ground.

Unit: Ohm (Ω)

Symbol: R

Formula:

R = V / I

Where:

R = Resistance  
V = Voltage  
I = Current

---

# Importance of Low Earthing Resistance

Low earthing resistance is very important for safety.

If resistance is low:

• Current flows easily  
• System is safe

If resistance is high:

• Current cannot flow easily  
• Electric shock risk increases

---

# Standard Values of Earthing Resistance

Recommended values:

• Domestic wiring → less than 5 Ω  
• Industrial systems → less than 1 Ω  
• Power stations → less than 0.5 Ω

Lower resistance means better earthing.

---

# Factors Affecting Earthing Resistance

Earthing resistance depends on several factors:

---

## 1. Soil Type

Wet soil → Low resistance  
Dry soil → High resistance

---

## 2. Soil Moisture

More moisture → Better earthing

---

## 3. Depth of Electrode

More depth → Lower resistance

---

## 4. Soil Temperature

High temperature → High resistance

---

# Earth Tester (Earth Megger)

Earth tester is an instrument used to measure earthing resistance.

Purpose:

• Measure earth resistance  
• Check earthing quality  
• Ensure electrical safety

---

# Parts of Earth Tester

Main parts include:

• Earth electrode terminal (E)  
• Current terminal (C)  
• Potential terminal (P)  
• Display meter

---

# Working of Earth Tester

Earth tester works on voltage and current measurement principle.

It measures resistance using formula:

R = V / I

---

# Procedure to Measure Earthing Resistance

Step 1: Connect earth tester to earth electrode

Step 2: Insert auxiliary electrodes in soil

Step 3: Connect wires properly

Step 4: Switch ON tester

Step 5: Read resistance value

---

# Interpretation of Earth Tester Reading

If resistance is:

• 0 – 2 Ω → Excellent earthing  
• 2 – 5 Ω → Good earthing  
• Above 5 Ω → Poor earthing

Poor earthing must be corrected.

---

# Advantages of Earthing

• Prevents electric shock  
• Protects equipment  
• Prevents fire accidents  
• Ensures safety  
• Improves system reliability

# Applications of Earthing

Used in:

• Houses  
• Industries  
• Power stations  
• Electrical installations

---

# Maintenance of Earthing System

Proper maintenance is required:

• Regular inspection  
• Check resistance  
• Maintain soil moisture

---

# Conclusion

Earthing is an essential safety system used in electrical installations to protect human life and equipment. It provides a safe path for fault current to flow into ground. Earthing resistance must be low for effective protection. Earth tester is used to measure earthing resistance and ensure proper functioning of earthing system. Proper earthing prevents electric shock, protects equipment, and ensures safe electrical operation.

# Introduction

Electricity is essential in industries, homes, and power systems. However, improper handling of electricity can cause electric shock, burns, injury, or death. Electrical shock occurs when electric current passes through the human body. The severity of shock depends on several important parameters such as current, voltage, resistance, duration, current path, and frequency.

Understanding these parameters helps in preventing electrical accidents and ensuring safety.

# Definition of Electrical Shock

Electrical shock is the effect produced when electric current passes through the human body, causing injury, burns, or death.

Shock occurs when:

• Human body completes electrical circuit  
• Person touches live electrical part

---

# Parameters Affecting Electrical Shock Hazard

There are six main parameters:

---

# 1. Magnitude of Current (Most Important Parameter)

Current is the amount of electric charge flowing through the body.

Unit: Ampere (A)

Higher current → More dangerous

Effects of current on human body:

|Current Level|Effect|
|---|---|
|1 mA|Slight sensation|
|5 mA|Mild shock|
|10 mA|Painful shock|
|30 mA|Muscle control loss|
|50–100 mA|Heart disturbance|
|Above 100 mA|Fatal (death possible)|

Conclusion:

Higher current increases severity of shock.

---

# 2. Voltage

Voltage is the force that pushes electric current through the body.

Unit: Volt (V)

Higher voltage → Higher current → More danger

Examples:

• 12 V → Low risk  
• 110 V → Dangerous  
• 230 V → Very dangerous  
• 440 V → Extremely dangerous

Conclusion:

Higher voltage increases shock risk.

---

# 3. Resistance of Human Body

Human body offers resistance to current flow.

Unit: Ohm (Ω)

Resistance depends on:

• Skin condition  
• Moisture level  
• Contact area

Examples:

• Dry skin → High resistance (less dangerous)  
• Wet skin → Low resistance (more dangerous)

Typical resistance values:

• Dry skin → 100,000 Ω  
• Wet skin → 1,000 Ω

Conclusion:

Lower resistance increases shock severity.

---

# 4. Duration of Contact (Time)

Duration is the time for which current flows through the body.

Longer duration → More damage

Effects:

• Short contact → Minor injury  
• Long contact → Severe injury or death

Example:

Touching live wire for long time increases danger.

Conclusion:

Longer exposure increases shock severity.

---

# 5. Path of Current Through Body

Current path is the route taken by current through the body.

Danger depends on which organs are affected.

Dangerous paths:

• Hand to hand  
• Hand to leg  
• Hand to chest

Most dangerous path:

Through heart → Causes heart failure

Example diagram:

```id="nq3r0b"
Hand → Heart → Leg
(Most dangerous path)
```

Conclusion:

Current passing through heart is most dangerous.

---

# 6. Frequency of Current

Frequency is number of cycles per second.

Unit: Hertz (Hz)

AC frequency (50 Hz) is more dangerous than DC.

Reason:

AC affects heart rhythm.

Example:

Domestic supply frequency = 50 Hz

Conclusion:

Higher frequency increases shock danger.

---

# Additional Factors Affecting Electrical Shock

Other factors include:

• Contact area  
• Environmental conditions  
• Type of current (AC or DC)

---

# Effects of Electrical Shock

Electric shock can cause:

• Burns  
• Muscle contraction  
• Heart failure  
• Nervous system damage  
• Death

---

# Prevention of Electrical Shock

Safety measures include:

• Proper earthing  
• Insulation of wires  
• Use of protective equipment  
• Regular inspection  
• Use of circuit breakers

---

# Importance of Understanding Shock Parameters

Understanding parameters helps:

• Prevent accidents

• Improve electrical safety  
• Reduce fatalities

---

# Applications

Used in:

• Electrical industries  
• Power plants  
• Construction sites  
• Electrical maintenance

---

# Conclusion

Electrical shock hazard depends on parameters such as current, voltage, resistance, duration of contact, current path, and frequency. Among these, current is the most important factor. Higher current, higher voltage, longer contact time, and current passing through the heart increase danger. Proper safety measures such as earthing, insulation, and protective equipment help prevent electrical shock and ensure safety.

# Lethal Dose and Lethal Concentration (20 Marks)

# Introduction

In industrial safety, toxicology is the study of harmful effects of chemicals on human health. Some chemicals can cause serious injury or death when they enter the human body. To measure the toxicity of such substances, two important terms are used: Lethal Dose (LD) and Lethal Concentration (LC).

These terms help in determining the dangerous levels of chemicals and setting safety standards to protect workers and environment.

---

# Definition of Lethal Dose (LD)

Lethal Dose is the amount of toxic substance that causes death when it enters the human body.

It is commonly expressed as LD₅₀.

LD₅₀ means the dose that causes death in 50% of test animals.

---

# Unit of Lethal Dose

The unit of lethal dose is:

mg/kg (milligram per kilogram of body weight)

---

# Explanation of Lethal Dose

Lethal dose measures toxicity based on the amount of substance entering the body.

The substance may enter through:

• Mouth (ingestion)  
• Skin contact  
• Injection

Lower LD₅₀ value → More toxic substance  
Higher LD₅₀ value → Less toxic substance

---

# Example of Lethal Dose

Example 1:

Potassium cyanide has very low LD₅₀ → Extremely dangerous

Example 2:

Common salt has high LD₅₀ → Less toxic

Example calculation:

If LD₅₀ = 10 mg/kg  
For 60 kg person:

Lethal dose = 10 × 60 = 600 mg

---

# Definition of Lethal Concentration (LC)

Lethal Concentration is the concentration of toxic substance present in air or water that causes death.

It is commonly expressed as LC₅₀.

LC₅₀ means concentration causing death in 50% of test animals.

---

# Unit of Lethal Concentration

Units include:

• ppm (parts per million)  
• mg/m³ (milligram per cubic meter)

---

# Explanation of Lethal Concentration

Lethal concentration measures toxicity based on concentration in environment.

Substance enters body through:

• Inhalation (breathing)

Lower LC₅₀ value → More dangerous  
Higher LC₅₀ value → Less dangerous

---

# Example of Lethal Concentration

Example 1:

Carbon monoxide gas causes death at high concentration.

Example 2:

Chlorine gas is toxic at high concentration.

---

# Difference Between Lethal Dose and Lethal Concentration

|Feature|Lethal Dose (LD)|Lethal Concentration (LC)|
|---|---|---|
|Definition|Amount causing death|Concentration causing death|
|Entry method|Ingestion, skin, injection|Inhalation|
|Unit|mg/kg|ppm or mg/m³|
|Example|Poison ingestion|Toxic gas inhalation|

---

# Factors Affecting Toxicity

Several factors affect lethal dose and concentration:

---

## 1. Body Weight

Higher body weight → Higher lethal dose required

---

## 2. Exposure Duration

Longer exposure → More dangerous

---

## 3. Chemical Properties

Highly toxic chemicals require smaller dose.

---

## 4. Health Condition

Weak individuals are more vulnerable.

---

# Importance in Industrial Safety

Lethal dose and concentration help in:

• Determining toxic limits  
• Setting safety standards  
• Protecting workers  
• Preventing accidents

---

# Applications

Used in:

• Chemical industries  
• Pharmaceutical industries  
• Environmental safety  
• Toxicology studies

---

# Safety Measures to Prevent Toxic Exposure

Safety measures include:

• Use of protective equipment  
• Proper ventilation  
• Proper chemical storage  
• Safety training

---

# Advantages of Using LD and LC Values

• Helps identify toxic substances  
• Helps establish safety limits  
• Helps prevent chemical accidents  
• Protects workers and environment

---

# Conclusion

Lethal Dose and Lethal Concentration are important measures used to determine toxicity of substances. Lethal dose measures amount of substance causing death, while lethal concentration measures concentration causing death through inhalation. These values help establish safety standards, prevent accidents, and protect workers and environment in industries.

# Definition of IDLH

IDLH stands for Immediately Dangerous to Life or Health.

IDLH is the concentration of a toxic substance that can cause immediate death or serious health effects.

---

# Features of IDLH

• Causes death immediately  
• Causes permanent health damage  
• Dangerous even with short exposure  
• Requires emergency protection

---

# Example of IDLH

Example:

Carbon monoxide IDLH value = 1200 ppm

Exposure above this level can cause death.

---

# Importance of IDLH

IDLH helps in:

• Emergency planning  
• Selection of protective equipment  
• Protecting workers

---

# Definition of STEL

STEL stands for Short-Term Exposure Limit.

STEL is the maximum concentration of a toxic substance that a worker can be exposed to for a short period (usually 15 minutes) without harmful effects.

---

# Features of STEL

• Exposure allowed for 15 minutes  
• Prevents short-term health effects  
• Exposure should not exceed limit

---

# Example of STEL

Example:

Ammonia STEL = 35 ppm

Exposure beyond this level causes irritation.

---

# Importance of STEL

STEL helps in:

• Preventing short-term health problems  
• Protecting workers from sudden exposure

---

# Definition of TLV

TLV stands for Threshold Limit Value.

TLV is the maximum concentration of a substance that a worker can be exposed to for 8 hours per day without harmful effects.

---

# Features of TLV

• Safe exposure level  
• Used for normal working conditions  
• Based on 8-hour workday

---

# Example of TLV

Example:

Carbon monoxide TLV = 50 ppm

Exposure below this level is considered safe.

---

# Types of TLV

There are three types:

---

## 1. TLV-TWA (Time Weighted Average)

Average exposure for 8 hours.

---

## 2. TLV-STEL

Short-term exposure limit.

---

## 3. TLV-C (Ceiling Limit)

Maximum exposure that should never be exceeded.

---

|Purpose|Normal working safety|Short-term safety|Emergency safety|
